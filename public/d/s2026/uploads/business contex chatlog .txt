  Think of it as two products that work together:

  Fabric Intelligence — $2k/month
  - Unlimited projects in discovery/design phase
  - Intelligence Layer (codebase analysis, pattern detection, risk scoring)
  - AI copilots for problem discovery, solution design, spec writing
  - Unlimited users

  Fabric Build — $3-8k/month (tiered by execution capacity)
  - Everything in Intelligence
  - Autonomous execution (projects go from spec → PR)
  - Build capacity: 150-500 hrs/month
  - Governance, audit trails, quality gates

  Combined paths:
  - Intelligence only: $2k/month (entry point, lands the account)
  - Intelligence + Build Small: $5k/month (10-20 features/month)
  - Intelligence + Build Growth: $10k/month (30-50 features/month)

  Why this structure is compelling

  It creates a natural land-and-expand motion. A skeptical VP Eng starts with Intelligence at $2k/month. PMs use it for discovery and design. The Intelligence Layer indexes their repos and starts learning patterns. Within a
  month, they have 10 fully specced projects sitting there ready to build. The PM says "why aren't we building these?" The upgrade to Build sells itself. You didn't have to convince anyone — the pipeline of ready-to-build
  projects creates internal pressure.

  It separates the switching cost from the value delivery. Intelligence is where the compounding moat lives — the codebase learning, the pattern detection, the institutional knowledge. By making that the base tier, you ensure
  every customer builds switching cost from day one, even before they start building. A customer on Intelligence-only for three months has already accumulated codebase knowledge they don't want to lose.

  It makes the value of each tier legible. "We pay $2k for the AI to understand our codebase and help us plan. We pay $3-8k more for it to actually build stuff." That's a sentence a VP Eng can say to their CFO.

  It solves your cost alignment problem. Planning is cheap to serve → low price, unlimited usage, maximum adoption. Building is expensive to serve → higher price, tiered by capacity, tied directly to value delivered.

  It doesn't penalize exploration. PMs can create 50 projects, explore aggressively, kill 30 of them, refine 15, and build 5. They never felt a meter ticking while they were thinking. The meter only starts when they say "build
  this." That's exactly where you want the pricing psychology.

  ---
  The risk to watch

  The gap between "planned" and "built" could become a negative signal if it gets too wide. If a customer has 40 planned projects and is only building 5, either:
  - They're getting value from the planning side (good — but you're undermonetizing)
  - The build quality isn't good enough to trust (bad — product problem)
  - They can't justify the build tier cost (bad — pricing problem)

  You'd want to track the plan→build conversion rate as a health metric. Healthy customer: 30-50% of planned projects get built. Unhealthy: under 10%.

❯ ok great. and what are some comparables that we can point to as to why these numbers make sense. Github and Linear are way cheaper pieces of software. 

⏺ Right — GitHub and Linear are the wrong comps, and you'll get that question. Here's how to frame it.

  Why GitHub/Linear are wrong comparisons

  GitHub and Linear are tools humans use to do their work. They make developers more organized or give them a better editor. The developer still does the work. Pricing follows a utility model: $10-20/seat/month, volume play,
  low friction.

  Fabric does the work. The output isn't "a developer had a better day." The output is a shipped feature that would have cost $1,000-5,000 in developer time. That's a different pricing category entirely.

  The rule of thumb: tools that help people work are priced like software. Things that replace work are priced like labor.

  The right comparables

  1. AI agents that do work autonomously

  Devin (Cognition) — $500/month per seat
  - Autonomous AI developer
  - Does execution only, no discovery/design lifecycle
  - Per-seat, so a team of 10 PMs using it = $5k/month anyway
  - Fabric does more (full lifecycle) and prices at the workspace level

  Factory AI — ~$5-10k/month estimated
  - AI agents for code generation
  - Similar autonomous execution positioning
  - Enterprise-focused

  The pattern: Autonomous AI that produces work output prices at $500+/seat or $5k+/workspace. The market has already established that "AI that does the work" is 10-50x the price of "AI that helps you do the work."

  2. Platforms that replace engineering capacity

  Retool / OutSystems / Mendix (enterprise low-code) — $5-15k/month
  - Let non-developers build internal tools and apps
  - Limited to simple CRUD / internal tooling
  - Fabric builds in your actual production codebase, not a walled garden
  - Same buyer (VP Eng wanting to reduce engineering bottleneck), same price range, much broader capability

  Vercel / Netlify (enterprise tier) — $5-20k/month
  - Platform that automates deployment and infrastructure
  - Automates one phase of the lifecycle (deploy)
  - Fabric automates a much larger chunk (discover → design → build → ship)

  3. Engineering intelligence / productivity platforms

  Jellyfish — $5-15k/month
  - Engineering management platform
  - Gives VP Eng visibility into velocity and allocation
  - Purely analytics — tells you what's happening, doesn't do anything about it
  - Fabric gives you the visibility AND does the work

  LinearB — $3-8k/month
  - Dev workflow analytics and automation
  - Measures cycle time, review time, etc.
  - Same buyer, same budget line, but passive (metrics) vs. active (execution)

  Pluralsight Flow — $5-10k/month
  - Engineering intelligence
  - Dashboards about developer productivity
  - Again: tells you about the problem, doesn't solve it

  4. QA/testing automation

  Datadog — $5-25k/month for mid-size teams
  - Monitoring and observability
  - Doesn't write code, doesn't fix problems — just alerts you
  - Engineering teams pay this happily because the alternative is downtime

  Snyk / Sonar (enterprise) — $3-10k/month
  - Security scanning and code quality
  - Finds problems, doesn't fix them
  - Fabric finds AND fixes

  5. The real comp: fractional labor

  This is the strongest framing for the buyer conversation:

  Contract developer — $15-25k/month
  - One person, working hours only, ramp time, management overhead
  - Fabric: multiple features shipping in parallel, overnight, no management, compounds over time

  Dev agency — $10-50k per feature
  - Doesn't know your codebase, long handoff cycles, inconsistent quality
  - Fabric: learns your patterns, instant context, consistent output

  Offshore team (3-5 devs) — $15-30k/month
  - Communication overhead, timezone friction, quality variance
  - Fabric: zero communication overhead, available 24/7, quality improves monthly

  ---
  The pitch on pricing

  When someone says "GitHub is $20/seat and Linear is $10/seat, why are you $5k/month," the answer is:

  "GitHub gives your developers a place to store code. Linear gives them a place to track work. We ship the work. The comparison isn't GitHub — it's the $15k/month developer you're trying to hire, or the $10k feature you're
  quoting to an agency. At $5k/month, we're a third the cost of one developer and we ship more features than they would."

  Or even shorter:

  "We're not a developer tool. We're a developer. The pricing reflects that."

  ---
  Anchoring the conversation

  The order you want the buyer thinking about comparisons:

  1. First anchor: headcount — "One developer is $15k/month. We're $5k."
  2. Second anchor: agencies — "An agency charges $10k per feature. We charge $5k/month for 15-40 features."
  3. Third anchor: other AI agent products — "Devin is $500/seat. For a team of 10, that's $5k/month with no intelligence layer, no discovery, no governance."
  4. Never let them anchor on: GitHub, Linear, Jira, Notion — those are tools, not labor.

  The $5k/month price should feel cheap by the time you've set these anchors. And it is — that's the point.

❯ ok great. what questions should i be asking early customers / MVP users / potential customers?                                                                                                                                   
                                                                                                 
⏺ Questions for early customers / prospects                                                                                                                                                                                        
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                 
  Before they use the product                                                                                                                                                                                                      
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                 
  Understanding their current pain:                                                                                                                                                                                                
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                   
  - "Walk me through what happens from when you identify a customer problem to when a fix is in production." You want the full lifecycle with all the handoffs, waits, and friction. Listen for where things stall and who's       
  waiting on whom.
  - "How many features are in your backlog right now? How old is the oldest one?" Quantifies the problem. If they say "6 months" that's pain. If they say "2 years" that's a company that's learned to live with it — different
  sell.
  - "When was the last time you killed a good idea because you didn't have engineering capacity for it?" This surfaces the hidden cost — the stuff that never gets built. They won't have a number for this but the stories are
  revealing.
  - "How do your PMs and engineers hand off work today? What gets lost in that handoff?" You're testing whether the PM-to-engineering gap is real and painful for them specifically. If they say "it works fine" they're not your
  customer.
  - "What are your developers spending time on that they shouldn't be?" Listen for: CRUD, boilerplate, admin panels, simple integrations. That's your wedge.
  - "Are your developers using AI tools today? Which ones? Do you have any visibility into what they're doing with them?" Tests the shadow AI angle and whether governance matters to this buyer.

  During pilot / first use

  Testing product-market fit:

  - "What did you try to use Fabric for first, and why that?" Reveals what they think the product is for. If it's different from what you intended, that's critical signal — either they're wrong or you are.
  - "Was there a moment where Fabric surprised you — either positively or negatively?" The positive surprises tell you what to double down on. The negative ones tell you where expectations don't match reality.
  - "What did you have to change about how you work to use Fabric?" If the answer is "a lot," adoption will be hard even if the product is good. If the answer is "not much, it fit into what we already do," you've got something.
  - "Show me a project where Fabric worked well. Now show me one where it didn't." Don't let them just show you the wins. The failure cases tell you where the product boundary is and whether customers understand it.
  - "How much time did you spend on the spec before submitting it to build?" If they're spending 3 hours writing specs, the copilot isn't doing its job. If they're spending 20 minutes and getting good results, the
  discovery/design flow is working.
  - "When the PR came back, what was your first reaction? What did you check first?" Tells you whether they trust the output and what trust-building looks like for them.

  Testing value perception

  - "If I took Fabric away tomorrow, what would you do instead?" The best answer is a pained look. If they shrug, you don't have product-market fit yet. This is the single most important question.
  - "Would you pay $5k/month for this? Why or why not?" Ask it directly. Don't dance around it. The reasons behind "no" are more valuable than the "no" itself.
  - "What would this need to do for you to be upset if we raised the price to $10k/month, but you'd still pay it?" This tells you what "must-have" looks like vs. "nice-to-have." It also anchors them on the value, not the cost.
  - "Who else at your company would use this if it worked well?" Tests whether this is a single-user tool or an organizational platform. You need the answer to be multiple roles (PMs, eng leads, etc.) for the workspace pricing
  to make sense.
  - "How would you explain what Fabric does to your CEO in one sentence?" Their answer is better positioning copy than anything you'll write yourself. If they can't explain it, your messaging isn't landing.

  Testing the lifecycle thesis specifically

  - "Before Fabric, where did you do problem discovery and solution design? What tools?" You need to know what you're replacing or augmenting. If they say "Google Docs and gut feel," your discovery features are a step change.
  If they say "we have a whole research ops team," you need to fit into that workflow.
  - "Did you use the discovery/design side, the build side, or both? Which was more valuable?" Critical for the pricing split. If everyone says "build," you might be overthinking the Intelligence tier. If they say "honestly the
   planning side changed how we think about problems," you've got a two-product company.
  - "Has Fabric changed what you decide to build, or just how fast you build it?" "How fast" = productivity tool. "What we build" = strategic tool. The latter is a much bigger business. You want to hear: "We're testing ideas we
   never would have bothered with before."

  Understanding the buyer

  - "Whose budget does this come out of?" Engineering? Product? A shared "AI/automation" budget? This tells you how to sell and who to sell to.
  - "What would need to be true for you to roll this out to your whole product team?" Surfaces the real objections — security review, procurement process, proof of ROI, champion buy-in. These are the things that kill deals
  after a successful pilot.
  - "Who would block this purchase internally, and why?" Engineering leadership might feel threatened. Security might worry about code access. Finance might not have a budget category for it. Name the blocker so you can address
   it.

  ---
  The meta-questions you're actually trying to answer

  Everything above ladders into five things you need to know:

  1. Is the PM-engineering bottleneck real and painful enough to pay to solve? (Or do they just complain about it and live with it?)
  2. Is the full lifecycle — discover, design, build — the right product, or do they only care about one piece? (If they only want the build side, you're competing with Devin. If they want the full lifecycle, you're in a
  category of one.)
  3. Does the product work well enough on their codebase to justify the price? (Complex real-world codebases are the test, not demos.)
  4. Is this a single-PM tool or does it spread to the whole organization? (Single PM = $500/month tool. Whole org = $5-10k/month platform.)
  5. After using it, can they go back to how things were before? (If yes, it's a vitamin. If no, it's a painkiller. You need painkiller.)
